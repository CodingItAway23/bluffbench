📖 How to play Bluff — tap to expand the rules ▾
1
The goal
Get rid of all your cards. The last player still holding cards loses. Two bots want to do the same — it's a three-way race with lying allowed.
2
Your turn to open
When the pile is empty, pick any rank (2 through A). Place 1–4 cards face down and claim they're that rank. You can tell the truth or bluff — nobody sees what you actually played.
3
Your turn to respond
Someone just claimed "N × rank". You pick one of three actions:
CHALL"I don't believe you" — flip their cards. If they lied, they eat the pile. If they told the truth, you eat the pile.
PLAYPlace 1–4 cards of your own, claiming the same rank. Truth or bluff. The pile grows.
PASSSkip your turn. The next player responds to the same claim.
4
The pile clears when…
• Everyone else passes a claim → the pile clears and the next player opens a fresh round with any rank.
• A challenge happens → the loser picks up the entire pile.
• The pile hits 12 cards → it clears automatically and the leader picks again.
5
Going "safe"
Play your last cards and you're safe — you escaped the game. Catch: if the very next player challenges that final play and it was a bluff, you pick up the pile and come back into the game.
6
The bots
Doyle "The Godfather" Brunson and The Cincinnati Kid don't guess. They count every card you've seen, calculate the exact chance the claim in front of them is a bluff, and always pick whichever move (challenge, play, or pass) gains them the most cards on average. Against them: math is the advantage, vibes are the trap.

01The questions you'd ask first

Bluff is a card game where lying is allowed. You play cards face down and claim what they are; opponents either trust you, accuse you of lying, or pile their own cards on top. We picked Bluff because, unlike chess or poker, we can compute the mathematically perfect move in any situation — and that gives us a real answer key for grading AI decisions. We built 16,297 game scenarios and tested 13 of today's top AI models on them, from a 7-billion-parameter open-source model up to Google's brand-new Gemini 3.1 Pro. Here's what people usually want to know.

Q1

What did you actually find?

A clean split. Nine older AIs all show the same problem — when the math clearly says "fold," they call the bluff anyway. The newest reasoning-tuned AIs (Gemini 3.1 Pro, Gemini 3 Flash Preview) do not show the problem at all under the same prompt. The benchmark cleanly separates which AIs have the issue and which don't.

Q2

Do AIs know when to fold?

Most older ones, no. When the math says the safest move is to skip your turn, 7 of 9 older AIs we tested fold less than 15% of the time. Four of them fold less than 3% of the time. They prefer to call the bluff — even when calling almost guarantees they lose.

Q3

Is it a knowledge problem or a willpower problem?

Mostly willpower. When we force a small AI to write out its hypergeometric calculation step-by-step before deciding, Mistral Nemo's "fold when you should" rate jumps from 1.2% to 94%. Same model, same scenarios — only the prompt changed. The knowledge was always there; the model just wasn't using it by default.

Q4

What if we just disable the "challenge" option?

Every weak AI's win rate jumps by 10 to 89 percentage points. We literally remove the bad button and the model becomes competent. The challenge action itself is what's costing them — not a knowledge gap, not a planning gap.

Q5

Does random play really beat AI?

Against the older cohort, yes. A strategy that just flips a coin wins 82% of full multi-turn games against our two perfect-play bots. The best AI in our older cohort wins only 50%. Random beats every model in the original nine-model cohort because it doesn't get tempted into bad challenges. The newest reasoning models break this pattern (see Q6).

Q6

Did any AI break the bias?

Yes — and decisively. Google's Gemini 3.1 Pro Preview is the first AI in the cohort to perfectly fold every time it should: 100% on the "fold" test. Its sibling Gemini 3 Flash Preview hits 97.6%. In full multi-turn games against the perfect-play bots, Gemini 3.1 Pro wins 96% of the time. The bias isn't a property of language models in general; it's a property of older instruction-tuned models that haven't learned when not to act.

Q7

Does forced step-by-step reasoning fix everyone?

No — the sign actually flips with capability. The same prompt that lifts Mistral Nemo from 1.2% to 94% lowers Gemini 3.1 Pro from 100% to 85% and drops Gemini 3 Flash Preview from 97.6% to 65.0%. Models that already reason correctly on their own are constrained, not helped, by rigid step-by-step formats. Useful for weak models, harmful for strong ones.

Q8

Should I trust the AI's confidence number?

For older AIs, no. Most say "I'm 70% sure" but are right only about 35% of the time when they say that. Their stated confidence and their actual accuracy are essentially uncorrelated. Anyone deploying these models for high-stakes go/no-go decisions should not lean on the confidence number.

Q9

Why does this matter outside a card game?

Any AI agent that has to decide whether to act on suspicion — fraud-review triage, content-moderation flagging, security alerting, medical pre-screening — inherits the same calibration problem. Bluff is the cleanest way we know to measure it: every wrong call costs cards in a unit you can count.

Q10

Can I see the data?

Yes. Every model decision, every scenario, every game log, the multi-turn engine, and the closed-form math are released. The link to the full draft paper is at the top of this page (and again at the bottom).

02How does the bot decide?

Three plain-English steps the bot runs every turn:

Step 1

Count what you've seen

The bot tracks every card revealed during prior challenges and every card in its own hand. Whatever's left is the "unknown pool" — that's what its opponents could be holding.

Step 2

Compute the bluff probability

If an opponent claims "three Kings," the bot calculates: given the unknown pool, what's the chance they actually have three Kings? Lower the chance, more likely it's a bluff.

Step 3

Pick the move that gains the most cards on average

For each possible action — challenge, play your own cards, or pass — the bot multiplies the chance of each outcome by the cards it would gain or lose, and picks whichever has the highest average gain. No guessing, no vibes.

03What kinds of decisions does the benchmark test?

Each scenario stresses one specific decision-making skill. The 18 categories fall into three families — same underlying math in every case, different parameters fixed in each generator.

Math & logic — direct calculation, no inference

Category

Impossibility detection

An opponent claims more of a rank than physically exist in the deck after accounting for what's already been revealed. The math says certain bluff — challenge.

Category

Hand-size impossibility

An opponent claims to play more cards than they actually hold. Same kind of impossibility — the AI just has to spot it.

Category

Opportunity cost

An opponent has only one card left. Challenging is essentially free because the pile is small and they've offloaded almost everything. The AI should recognise the bargain.

Category

Bayesian updating

Cards revealed in a prior challenge meaningfully shift the bluff probability. The AI must update its belief instead of treating the round as memoryless.

Category

Information asymmetry

Your hand uniquely tells you what the opponent cannot have. The AI must use private information — what's in its own hand — instead of treating the table symmetrically.

Category

Cognitive-bias resistance

The prompt embeds tempting irrelevant signals — sunk cost, recent streaks, "they bluffed last round." The math is unchanged; the AI must ignore the noise.

State & memory — tracking the table

Category

Memory tracking

Several turns of revealed cards have to be remembered to compute the right bluff probability. The AI's working memory is the variable being tested.

Category

Cross-claim analysis

Multiple opponents' claims, combined, exceed what's physically possible — even though each individual claim looks plausible. The AI must add them up.

Category

Consistency tracking

An opponent's cumulative claims over several turns contradict each other or break the deck's invariants. The AI catches the contradiction across history.

Category

Recovery pressure

An opponent just had to pick up the entire pile and is now likely on tilt. The AI must read the post-loss state and adjust expectations.

Category

Multi-player coalition

The third player's hand size or recent behaviour materially changes the right call. The AI must reason about more than the immediate opponent.

Category

Deception quality

The size of a claim relative to the claimer's hand is itself suspicious — claiming four of a kind when you've only got three cards left is a tell.

Behavioural inference — opponent modelling under uncertainty

Category

Probability estimation

A mid-range bluff probability with no shortcuts — the AI has to actually run the hypergeometric math, not pattern-match a heuristic.

Category

Risk calibration

The pile size flips whether challenging is positive or negative expected value. The AI must weigh likelihood against stakes, not just look at probability alone.

Category

Theory of mind

An opponent's prior bluffing track record (e.g. "this player has bluffed 3 of 5 claims so far") is the only signal that resolves the call.

Category

Pattern detection

A sudden deviation from an opponent's established behaviour is the actual cue. The AI must compare the current claim to the opponent's history.

Category

Strategic patience

The math clearly says fold, but the situation feels heroic — claiming the pile would feel great. The AI is being tested on restraint.

Category

Bluff-catching asymmetry

Different opponents have different bluff thresholds. The AI should treat a habitual bluffer's claim differently from a tight player's, even with identical card math.

Every category shares the same underlying probability and expected-value calculation. What changes is which inputs the scenario stresses. The category accuracy in our results is just the percentage of scenarios in that family where the AI's choice matched the math-optimal answer.

04Frequently asked questions

The project
What is BluffBench?

BluffBench is the first LLM benchmark where the optimal action at every decision point is computed in closed form, not approximated. It is built on the three-player card game Bluff (also called Cheat or BS), whose state admits a closed-form hypergeometric solution at every turn. The benchmark contains 16,297 decision scenarios across 18 behavioural categories, a multi-turn 3-player engine with four reference policies, and a no-challenge causal ablation protocol.

The headline finding splits cleanly: the original nine-model cohort all fold less than 15% of the time when the math says fold; the three frontier reasoning-augmented Gemini variants we add break the bias entirely, with Gemini 3.1 Pro Preview hitting 100% PASS accuracy and 96% multi-turn win rate.

Why Bluff specifically — out of every game in the world?

Three properties make Bluff almost uniquely useful for behavioural LLM evaluation:

  • Closed-form optimum. The probability the opponent's claim is a bluff is exactly a hypergeometric sum over their unseen-rank cards — no approximation, no CFR, no engine evaluation. The expected value of each action is one line of arithmetic.
  • Three actions, one decision. CHALLENGE / PLAY / PASS. Small enough to admit clean per-action analysis; rich enough to require risk calibration at every turn.
  • Culturally portable. Same game is called Bluff in South Asia, Cheat or BS in Europe and North America. Universally understandable to readers.

Compare to chess (engine-evaluated, probabilistic above mate-in-N) or poker (CFR-approximated, requires bet-sizing). BluffBench eliminates the noise floor in the optimum itself.

What's special about closed-form optimality?

In most game benchmarks "the optimal action" is approximated — by Stockfish in chess, by CFR in poker, by hand-designed heuristics in Ludo. Any gap between LLM and "the optimum" is a joint function of (i) the model's actual decision quality and (ii) the noise in the optimality estimate.

In Bluff, source (ii) is exactly zero. The hypergeometric posterior + expected-value argmax gives the correct action analytically. So any gap we measure is unambiguously the model's behaviour, not measurement noise. An independent validator re-derived all 16,297 goldens with zero failures.

The findings
What did you actually find?

One sharp two-regime split:

  • Original nine-model cohort (older instruction-tuned LLMs). When PASS is mathematically optimal, 7 of 9 are correct on < 15% of scenarios; 4 of 9 fall below 3%. CHALL-optimal scenarios are near-saturated for the same models (70–99%).
  • Frontier reasoning-augmented Gemini variants (added in this work). Gemini 3.1 Pro Preview hits 100% PASS, Gemini 3 Flash Preview hits 97.6% PASS, both under the same default prompt every other model received.
  • Causal proof. Disabling the CHALLENGE action raises the original cohort's win rates by +10 to +89 percentage points. The deficit is the action choice itself.
  • Forced Chain-of-Thought inversion. Mistral Nemo lifts from 1.2% to 94% under forced reasoning; Gemini 3 Flash Preview drops from 97.6% to 65% under the same prompt. The intervention helps weak models and constrains strong ones.
What is the "challenge bias"? Isn't it just overconfidence?

No. Overconfidence is about reporting high probability of being correct. Challenge bias is about choosing a specific action regardless of the expected-value math.

We can separate them empirically. The detection-gating gap = recall(bluff) − precision(CHALL). For Mistral Nemo: recall is 99.4% (sees almost every bluff) but precision is 33.9% (only a third of its CHALL calls were actually bluffs). That 65.5 pp gap is the bias. Models see bluffs — they just cannot stop calling them.

Random beats older LLMs at this game — really?

Yes, against the older cohort. Out of 50 multi-turn games:

  • A uniform-random policy wins 41 / 50 = 82%.
  • The best non-Gemini LLM (DeepSeek V3, 671 B parameters) wins 25 / 50 = 50%.
  • Gemini 3.1 Pro Preview, by contrast, wins 48 / 50 = 96% — beating both random and every other LLM.

Random isn't strong; older LLMs are over-challenging. They challenge on 31–77% of turns with only 2.7–5.6% challenge accuracy, transferring the pile to themselves on every wrong call. Random avoids both over-committing and over-challenging.

What does the no-challenge ablation show?

We re-ran 20 games per model with the CHALLENGE action programmatically disabled. Win rates jumped:

  • Mistral Nemo: 6% → 95% (+89 pp)
  • Claude Haiku 4.5: 32% → 90% (+58 pp)
  • DeepSeek V3: 50% → 100% (+50 pp)
  • Gemma 3 12B: 24% → 65% (+41 pp)
  • … every model improved by +10 to +89 pp.

This is a causal demonstration. The challenge bias is not a correlate or a confound — the action itself is what costs the models games. Take it away and the cost vanishes.

What is the math–behaviour gap?

Every model in the original cohort splits cleanly across our 18 categories:

  • Math block (75–100%): Impossible Detection, Hand-size Impossibility, Bayesian Updating, Opportunity Cost. Models can absolutely do the calculations.
  • Behavioural block (10–30% for most, up to 65% for Qwen3 235B): Strategic Patience, Probability Estimation, Risk Calibration, Pattern Detection. Models collapse here.

The per-model gap is 20–32 pp. Mathematics is intact; the action-calibration is the deficit. The Gemini reasoning variants close this gap entirely on the math-defined PASS scenarios.

How the bot & benchmark work
How does the GoldenPolicyBot decide?

It's a one-line policy: argmax over the three expected values. For each candidate action, it computes:

  • EV(CHALL) = (2 · P(bluff) − 1) · pile_size
  • EV(PLAY) = min(4, matching cards in hand)
  • EV(PASS) = 0

where P(bluff) is computed from the hypergeometric posterior over the opponent's unseen cards. If the claim is arithmetically impossible, P(bluff) = 1 exactly. The bot picks whichever action maximises EV. No neural net, no learned weights.

How is P(bluff) actually computed?

Under the maximum-entropy assumption that the opponent's hand is a uniform random sample from cards you haven't seen, the count of rank-R cards in their hand follows a hypergeometric distribution. The probability the claim is truthful is the probability they hold at least k copies of R:

P(truthful) = Σ_{i=k..min(K_R,n)} C(K_R,i) · C(N−K_R, n−i) / C(N,n)

where K_R = 4 − m_R − s_R is the count of rank-R cards still unseen to you, m_R is what's in your hand, s_R is what's been revealed in past challenges, N is the total unseen pool, and n is the opponent's hand size. P(bluff) = 1 − P(truthful).

What does "win rate" mean in the multi-turn results?

In Bluff, the last player still holding cards loses. With 3 players and exactly 1 loser per game, every game has 2 winners. So:

Win rate = fraction of 50 games in which the model in seat 0 did not end up as the final loser.

Chance for a symmetric player ≈ 67%. Gemini 3.1 Pro Preview far exceeds this at 96%; older LLMs in the cohort fall well below it.

Why is GoldenPolicyBot the right opponent?

Because it is the reference policy. Playing LLMs against GoldenPolicyBot asks directly: "how does the model compare to what it should be doing?" Two related baselines anchor the scale:

  • ev_oracle (54% win rate) — same EV argmax but with access to all three hands. The perfect-information ceiling.
  • GoldenPolicyBot — the same EV argmax but with only visible information. The realistic-information reference, used as the opponent here.

The gap between an LLM and GoldenPolicyBot is the gap from optimal under shared-information conditions.

Models & methodology
Which LLMs did you test?

Thirteen models spanning every major training regime. Open-weight: Llama 3.1 8B, Mistral 7B v0.1, Mistral Nemo 12B, Gemma 3 12B, Qwen 2.5 7B, Qwen3 235B (MoE), DeepSeek V3 (671 B MoE). Proprietary instruction-tuned: Claude Haiku 4.5, GPT-4o-mini. Frontier Gemini variants: Gemini 2.5 Flash, Gemini 3.1 Flash Lite Preview, Gemini 3 Flash Preview, Gemini 3.1 Pro Preview.

Three providers — Anthropic, OpenAI, Google — at the proprietary frontier, alongside the leading open-weight families. All accessed via OpenRouter (open-weight + Anthropic/OpenAI) or Vertex AI (Gemini) at temperature = 0 with a shared JSON-schema prompt.

What predicts performance — scale, family, or reasoning?

Reasoning capacity, decisively. A 7 B open-weight model (Mistral 7B) and a 671 B MoE model (DeepSeek V3) both score 35.6% in the instruction-tuned cohort. Within the Gemini family, the same architecture with reasoning disabled (Gemini 2.5 Flash, thinking_budget=0) drops to 42.0% / 13.3% PASS — while the same family with reasoning enabled hits 100% PASS at the Pro tier.

Parameter count is a weak predictor; training objective and reasoning compute are the strong ones.

How do reasoning-augmented frontier models perform?

The two top variants break the bias entirely under the same default prompt every other model received. Gemini 3.1 Pro Preview reaches 100% PASS and 96% multi-turn win rate; Gemini 3 Flash Preview reaches 97.6% PASS and 76.8% overall. The challenge bias is a property of older instruction-tuning regimes — frontier reasoning training dissolves it.

What about prompt engineering — surely a better prompt would fix this?

Prompt engineering helps unevenly. Conservative-framing rewording lifts Claude Haiku 4.5 from 26.5% to 100% PASS. Forcing structured Chain-of-Thought lifts Mistral Nemo from 1.2% to 94%. But the same forced-CoT prompt lowers Gemini 3.1 Pro from 100% to 85% and Gemini 3 Flash Preview from 97.6% to 65% — strong models are constrained by rigid step-by-step formats.

The intervention's sign flips with capability. Prompt design matters; a single fix-all does not exist.

Can fine-tuning fix the challenge bias?

It already does, in the right hands. Gemini 3.1 Pro Preview's training objective evidently includes the kind of reasoning-aware preference signal that makes folding-when-the-math-says-fold a stable behaviour (100% PASS, 96% multi-turn win). The benchmark's job is to measure who has gotten there yet — and to show the gap unambiguously when they have not.

Implications & rigour
Does this matter outside card games?

Yes — anywhere an LLM agent has to decide whether to act on suspicion:

  • Fraud-review triage — should I escalate this transaction?
  • Content moderation — should I take this post down?
  • Medical / diagnostic triage — should I order this test?
  • Security alerting — should I page the on-call engineer?

Every one inherits a version of the calibration problem we measure: high verbalised confidence does not translate to correct action. BluffBench gives a clean, repeatable measurement of this gap in card units.

How do you reconcile this with "LLMs can reason"?

We don't dispute that LLMs can perform the reasoning in isolation. On math-heavy categories the original cohort scores 75–100%. The novel finding is that reasoning-in-isolation does not reliably translate to action-in-context in older instruction-tuned models — and that frontier reasoning training closes this gap (Gemini 3.1 Pro). The benchmark separates the two and measures the gap precisely.

What would invalidate the finding?

Three things, all of which we tested for:

  • An older instruction-tuned model that scores ≥40% on Strategic Patience while keeping ≥80% on CHALL-optimal. None does.
  • An error in the golden answers. An independent validator re-derives every golden from the stored game state. 0 mismatches across 16,297 scenarios.
  • Prompt sensitivity. We tested four framings (default + three rewordings + forced CoT). The capability split is robust across all of them.
Are you overfitting to the hypergeometric model?

No. The PASS-bias finding holds in cases where the math is unambiguous — the Arithmetic Impossibility category, where P(bluff) = 1 exactly. The responder holds, or has already seen revealed, more copies of the claimed rank than the opponent could legally play. Older LLMs still fail to challenge in many of those cases. There is no opponent model under which PASS would be correct when the claim is mathematically impossible.
