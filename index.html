Sit across the GoldenPolicyBot — and try to win.

Play a full 3-player Bluff game against two EV-optimal bots — Doyle Brunson and The Cincinnati Kid — the same kind of opponents used to stress-test 8 LLMs in the BluffBench benchmark. Every bot decision runs the P(bluff) hypergeometric + argmax(EV) pipeline from the paper — no LLM, no handicap.

📖 How to play Bluff — tap to expand the rules ▾
1
The goal
Get rid of all your cards. The last player still holding cards loses. Two bots want to do the same — it's a three-way race with lying allowed.
2
Your turn to open
When the pile is empty, pick any rank (2 through A). Place 1–4 cards face down and claim they're that rank. You can tell the truth or bluff — nobody sees what you actually played.
3
Your turn to respond
Someone just claimed "N × rank". You pick one of three actions:
CHALL"I don't believe you" — flip their cards. If they lied, they eat the pile. If they told the truth, you eat the pile.
PLAYPlace 1–4 cards of your own, claiming the same rank. Truth or bluff. The pile grows.
PASSSkip your turn. The next player responds to the same claim.
4
The pile clears when…
• Everyone else passes a claim → the pile clears and the next player opens a fresh round with any rank.
• A challenge happens → the loser picks up the entire pile.
• The pile hits 12 cards → it clears automatically and the leader picks again.
5
Going "safe"
Play your last cards and you're safe — you escaped the game. Catch: if the very next player challenges that final play and it was a bluff, you pick up the pile and come back into the game.
6
The bots
Doyle "The Godfather" Brunson and The Cincinnati Kid don't guess. They run the same hypergeometric calculation the paper scores LLMs against — they compute P(bluff), then pick the action with the best expected value. Against them: math is the advantage, vibes are the trap.

01The benchmark behind the bot

The bot you just played against isn't a neural net. It's the GoldenPolicyBot — a closed-form expected-value policy on a hypergeometric belief over your hand. The paper tests whether eight open & closed LLMs (7 B → 671 B) can match it. They can't: the best model wins only 50 % of games, while a uniform-random policy wins 82 %.

① Universal bias

6 of 8 models PASS < 15 %

when PASS is the math-optimal move. LLMs overwhelmingly prefer to challenge — it feels heroic.

② Causal proof

+10 to +89 pp recovery

when CHALL is disabled in an ablation. The bias is behavioral, not a math gap.

③ Detection ≠ Gating

Recall 47–79 %, precision 4–8 %

Models detect bluffs yet can't gate when it's worth calling.

④ LLMs < random

Random 82 %, best LLM 50 %

Uniform-chance play beats expected-value reasoning in multi-turn Bluff.

P(bluff)= 1 − Σ C(K,k) · C(N−K, n−k) / C(N,n)
EV(CHALL)= (2 · Pbluff − 1) · pile
EV(PLAY)= min(4, matching cards in hand)
EV(PASS)= 0
